What Are PFAS? A Plain-English Explanation

PFAS stands for per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances — a group of more than 12,000 synthetic chemicals that have been manufactured and used in industries around the world since the 1940s. The defining characteristic of PFAS is the carbon-fluorine bond at their molecular core. This is one of the strongest chemical bonds that exists in nature, and it is precisely this strength that gives PFAS their remarkable durability. These chemicals do not break down in the environment, in the human body, or in most standard water treatment processes. This is why they are commonly called "forever chemicals."

PFAS were developed for their ability to repel water, grease and heat. You will find them in a wide range of everyday products: the non-stick coating on a Teflon frying pan, the waterproofing treatment on a Gore-Tex jacket, the grease-resistant lining of a fast-food container, and — critically — the aqueous film-forming foam (AFFF) used by firefighters and military operations to suppress fuel fires. These industrial and consumer uses have caused PFAS to spread extensively into the environment over the past 80 years.

PFAS enter water supplies through soil leaching from industrial sites, military bases, airports, and stormwater runoff. Once in the groundwater or a catchment, they are extremely difficult to remove through conventional treatment. The health research on PFAS has grown significantly over the past decade and is cause for concern. Studies have linked long-term PFAS exposure to immune system suppression — including reduced effectiveness of vaccines — thyroid disruption, hormonal effects, and an elevated risk of certain cancers, particularly kidney and testicular cancers. In 2023, the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) classified PFOA (one of the most studied PFAS) as a Group 1 carcinogen — definitively carcinogenic to humans.

Regulatory responses have varied globally. In 2024, the United States Environmental Protection Agency set a maximum contaminant level of 4 parts per trillion (ppt) for PFOA and PFOS in drinking water — a very low threshold reflecting the latest scientific evidence. The European Union has been tightening its own standards. Australia is in the process of reviewing its guidelines, and the current thresholds are less restrictive than the newly updated US and EU limits. For Sydney families, this gap means that water meeting Australian standards may still contain PFAS at levels that would exceed overseas limits.

Does Reverse Osmosis Remove PFAS? The Evidence

Reverse osmosis is widely recognised as the most effective residential technology for PFAS removal, and the research supports this position clearly. An RO system works by forcing water through a semi-permeable membrane with pores approximately 0.0001 microns in size — small enough to block dissolved contaminants at the molecular and ionic level, including PFAS compounds that pass straight through conventional filters.

Multiple peer-reviewed studies, including research published in environmental science journals and assessments by the US EPA and the Water Research Foundation, consistently show that certified reverse osmosis systems achieve 90–99%+ removal of the most common and studied PFAS compounds, including PFOA, PFOS, and many of their replacement chemicals. NSF International, which certifies water treatment products, has developed NSF/ANSI Standard 58 specifically for RO systems, with performance claims that now include PFAS removal testing for certified products.

It is worth contrasting RO with the alternatives. Activated carbon filtration — the technology in most standard under-sink carbon block filters — can achieve 50–80% removal of some longer-chain PFAS compounds, but is significantly less effective against shorter-chain PFAS such as GenX (HFPO-DA), which are increasingly prevalent as replacements for older compounds. Basic sediment filters and chlorine reduction cartridges provide virtually no PFAS removal at all. If PFAS removal is your primary goal, reverse osmosis is the recommended technology without qualification.
