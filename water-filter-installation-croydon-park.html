Is Sydney Tap Water Safe to Drink? What Croydon Park Residents Should Know

Sydney tap water meets Australian Drinking Water Guidelines and is safe from a regulatory standpoint. Sydney Water tests thousands of samples annually and publishes detailed quality reports. However, "meeting guidelines" and "optimal quality" are different things.

The guidelines permit trace levels of various compounds, including:

  • Chlorine and chloramines — added for disinfection, cause taste and odour issues
  • Trihalomethanes (THMs) — disinfection byproducts formed when chlorine reacts with organic matter
  • PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) — found in some Sydney supply areas at trace levels
  • Microplastics — detected in most metropolitan water supplies globally, including Sydney
  • Lead — can leach from older household plumbing, particularly in pre-1970 properties
  • Sediment — pipe-related particles, especially in older mains

For most healthy adults, Sydney tap water poses no immediate health risk. But for families with young children, pregnant women, people with compromised immune systems, or anyone who simply wants the cleanest possible water at home, a quality filtration system provides meaningful peace of mind.

Reverse Osmosis in Croydon Park — Is It Worth It?

Reverse osmosis (RO) is the gold standard for drinking water filtration. Where a standard carbon filter removes chlorine, taste and odour, a reverse osmosis system removes up to 99% of dissolved contaminants — including fluoride, heavy metals, nitrates, bacteria, viruses and microplastics.

For Croydon Park families wanting the absolute cleanest drinking water, Jean-Paul's Pure Plus+ 5-Stage RO system ($840 installed) is the most popular choice. The Pure Premium 7-Stage ($1,180) adds alkaline remineralisation, returning beneficial minerals like calcium and magnesium to the filtered water after purification.

The trade-off with RO is that it filters water slowly — most systems produce a few litres per hour and store it in a small tank under the sink. For drinking and cooking, this is more than sufficient for most households.
